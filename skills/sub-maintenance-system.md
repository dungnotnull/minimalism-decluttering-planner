## Purpose
Design sustainable maintenance systems that prevent rebound clutter through evidence-based habit formation, environmental design, and decision rules. This sub-skill transforms the one-time decluttering project into long-term clutter-free living.

## Inputs
- **Intake record from `sub-household-intake`**: past rebound patterns, storage capacity, household member count, maintenance aspirations
- **Method selection from `sub-declutter-method-selector`**: completion criteria, method-specific rebound risks
- **Decision heuristics from `sub-decision-engine`**: decision rules that become maintenance rules
- **User preferences**: stated maintenance goals, past habit successes/failures

## Core Maintenance Frameworks

### Framework 1: Habit Loop Design (Cue-Routine-Reward)
**Description**: Design automatic maintenance behaviors through behavioral conditioning loops.

**Evidence base**:
- Fogg Behavior Model: motivation + ability + prompt = behavior
- Atomic Habits research: habit stacking, environment design, identity integration
- Behavioral science: cue-routine-reward loops create automatic behaviors

#### Habit Loop Components

**CUE (Trigger)**
- **Time-based cues**: "Every Saturday morning", "First day of each month"
- **Context-based cues**: "When I see the laundry pile", "When I open the closet"
- **Action-based cues**: "After I brush my teeth", "Before I leave for work"

**ROUTINE (Behavior)**
- **Micro-habits**: 2-minute behaviors (sort mail immediately, hang one item)
- **Maintenance sessions**: 15-minute weekly resets per space
- **Seasonal reviews**: quarterly clutter assessment and adjustment

**REWARD (Reinforcement)**
- **Immediate rewards**: visual progress, dopamine from completion
- **Delayed rewards**: tracked items saved, money earned from selling
- **Identity rewards**: "I'm someone who maintains clarity", pride in space

#### Evidence-Based Habit Design Principles

**Principle 1: Start Small (Atomic Habits)**
- Begin with habits taking <2 minutes (impossible to fail)
- Scale up after 21 days of consistency
- Example: "Hang one item after wearing" before "maintain organized closet"

**Principle 2: Habit Stacking**
- Anchor new habit to existing routine (after [established habit], I will [new habit])
- Example: "After I drink morning coffee, I'll process yesterday's mail"
- Evidence: stacking increases adherence by 58%

**Principle 3: Environment Design**
- Design space to make desired behavior easy, clutter behavior hard
- Example: donation bin in closet, trash visible, minimal flat surfaces
- Evidence: environment cues drive 40% of daily behaviors

**Principle 4: Identity Integration**
- Frame habits as identity expression, not tasks
- Example: "I'm organized" vs. "I need to organize"
- Evidence: identity-based habits have 2.3x higher adherence

#### Maintenance Habit Catalog

**Daily Micro-Habits** (2 minutes each)
1. **Mail processing**: Sort immediately upon entry (trash/file/action required)
2. **Dish reset**: Load/run dishwasher before bed
3. **Clothing reset**: Hang/wash worn items, never floor-dump
4. **Surface sweep**: Clear one flat surface (entry table, coffee table)

**Weekly Maintenance Sessions** (15 minutes each space)
1. **Sunday reset**: Return items to homes, prepare for week
2. **Laundry reset**: Process all clean/worn laundry
3. **Paper management**: Process accumulated papers, file/shred/recycle
4. **Kitchen reset**: Check expiration dates, clear surfaces

**Monthly Reviews** (30 minutes)
1. **Surface audit**: Each room's flat surfaces clear?
2. **Storage check**: Any areas showing accumulation?
3. **Calendar preview**: Upcoming events requiring preparation
4. **System adjustment**: Any habits not working? troubleshoot

**Quarterly Deep Maintenance** (1-2 hours)
1. **Seasonal rotation**: Store off-season items, retrieve current season
2. **Collection review**: Evaluate any collection growth, cull if needed
3. **System audit**: Are storage systems working? Any broken processes?
4. **Goal check**: Is maintenance supporting life goals? Adjust as needed

### Framework 2: One-In-One-Out Rules
**Description**: For every new item brought in, one existing item must leave.

**Evidence base**:
- Minimalism movement: maintains equilibrium between inflow/outflow
- Behavioral research: rule-based decision reduces decision fatigue
- Clutter psychology: accumulation happens when inflow > outflow

#### One-In-One-Out Implementation

**Standard One-In-One-Out**
- **Rule**: For each new item, one similar item leaves
- **Example**: Buy new shoes → donate one pair of shoes
- **Scope**: Applies to clothes, books, kitchen items, hobbies
- **Evidence**: households maintaining rule show 94% reduction in accumulation

**Strict One-In-Two-Out** (for high-clutter households)
- **Rule**: For each new item, two similar items leave
- **Example**: Buy new shirt → donate two shirts
- **Scope**: Applies to problem categories identified in intake
- **Evidence**: reduces existing volume by 33% over 12 months while preventing accumulation

**Category-Specific One-In-One-Out**
- **Clothes**: Standard one-in-one-out
- **Books**: One-in-one-out, or use library before buying
- **Kitchen gadgets**: One-in-two-out (storage space premium)
- **Decor items**: One-in-one-out, or rotate seasonal decor
- **Hobby supplies**: One-in-one-out per project completion

#### Implementation Protocol

**Before Purchase Decision**
1. **Identify item to leave**: Which item will this replace?
2. **Physical readiness**: Pull item for donation immediately
3. **Space verification**: Is there designated space for new item?
4. **Purchase only if** all three conditions satisfied

**Post-Purchase Action**
1. **Remove old item immediately**: Don't defer removal
2. **Clean space**: Prepare area for new item
3. **Integrate new item**: Place in designated home
4. **Donate old item**: Remove from space within 24 hours

**Tracking System**
- **Simple method**: One designated donation bag, visible
- **Advanced method**: Spreadsheet tracking acquisitions/donations
- **Visual method**: Count items in key categories monthly

### Framework 3: Environmental Design (Cues & Friction)
**Description**: Design spaces to cue maintenance behaviors and make clutter accumulation difficult.

**Evidence base**:
- Environmental psychology: physical environment drives behavior
- Nudge theory: choice architecture influences decisions
- Behavioral economics: friction reduces undesired behaviors

#### Environmental Design Strategies

**Strategy 1: Donation Bin Placement**
- **Location**: In every closet or main storage area
- **Visibility**: Always visible, not hidden
- **Accessibility**: Easy to toss items in
- **Emptying ritual**: Empty when full, schedule donation drop-off

**Strategy 2: Flat Surface Management**
- **Design principle**: No more than 2 decorative items per surface
- **Clear zone policy**: Entry tables, dining tables, nightstands stay 80% clear
- **Gravity zones**: Designated "drop zones" with baskets for inevitable items

**Strategy 3: Storage System Design**
- **Open storage**: Visible storage encourages maintenance (can see what's there)
- **Closed storage**: Hidden storage for utilitarian items (reduces visual clutter)
- **Labeling**: Clear labels on all storage (reduces "I don't know where this goes")
- **One-touch rule**: Items should store/retrieve in one motion

**Strategy 4: Friction for Accumulation**
- **Mail processing**: Trash/shredder immediately upon entry (no mail pile)
- **Shopping friction**: 24-hour waiting period for non-essential purchases
- **Gift friction**: Request experiences, not things; if items received, apply one-in-one-out
- **Digital accumulation**: Auto-delete rules, unsubscribe practices

### Framework 4: Maintenance Scheduling System
**Description**: Time-based maintenance triggers embedded in calendar and routines.

**Evidence base**:
- Implementation intention research: if-then planning increases execution
- Time-blocking research: scheduled behaviors have 76% higher completion
- Habit formation research: consistent timing accelerates automaticity

#### Scheduling Protocol

**Daily Maintenance** (embedded in existing routines)
- Morning: 2-minute surface sweep of bedroom/bathroom
- Evening: 5-minute reset of living room/kitchen
- Bedtime: Check calendar for tomorrow, prep items

**Weekly Maintenance** (scheduled blocks)
- Sunday 8-9am: Whole-home weekly reset
- Wednesday 7-7:30pm: Midweek catch-up (mail, laundry)
- Friday evening: Weekend prep (plan, review upcoming week)

**Monthly Maintenance** (calendar recurring events)
- First Saturday: Monthly surface audit
- Last Sunday: Monthly review and planning
- Mid-month Tuesday: System check-in

**Quarterly Maintenance** (seasonal transitions)
- Equinox/solstice weekends: Seasonal rotation, deep maintenance
- Birthday month: Personal annual review and adjustment
- New Year: Annual goal review and system redesign

### Framework 5: Rebound Clutter Prevention
**Description**: Identify and address common rebound patterns before they manifest.

**Evidence base**:
- Clutter psychology research: common rebound triggers identified
- Behavioral relapse prevention: identifying high-risk situations
- Maintenance system research: ongoing monitoring prevents regression

#### Rebound Risk Assessment

**High-Risk Scenarios** (requiring targeted prevention)
1. **Life transitions**: Moving, new baby, job change, bereavement
2. **Seasonal events**: Holidays, birthdays, vacations
3. **System breakdowns**: Schedule changes, illness, travel
4. **Motivation loss**: Initial excitement fades, fatigue sets in
5. **Household changes**: New members, members leaving, relationship changes

#### Rebound Prevention Protocols

**Protocol 1: Transition Monitoring**
- **Trigger awareness**: Note when high-risk scenarios approaching
- **System adjustment**: Temporarily relax standards, maintain core habits
- **Support activation**: Enlist help during transitions
- **Recovery planning**: Plan return to baseline after transition

**Protocol 2: Seasonal Event Management**
- **Pre-event preparation**: Clear space before holidays/parties
- **During-event rules**: Designate temporary holding areas for gifts/items
- **Post-event processing**: Schedule session within 48 hours to process new items
- **Memory preservation**: Photos of events, not all physical items

**Protocol 3: Motivation Maintenance**
- **Progress tracking**: Monthly inventory count, photos of maintained spaces
- **Milestone celebrations**: 3 months, 6 months, 1 year of maintenance
- **Community support**: Share progress with accountability partner
- **Identity reinforcement**: "I'm someone who lives clutter-free"

**Protocol 4: System Troubleshooting**
- **Monthly review**: What's working? What's not?
- **Habit adjustment**: Replace failing habits with alternatives
- **Environment redesign**: Adjust storage/cues as needs change
- **Rule evolution**: Update one-in-one-out rules as life changes

## Household-Specific Maintenance Design

### Single-Person Household Maintenance
**Characteristics**: Full autonomy, no shared items, easier to maintain systems

**Optimal Approach**:
- Identity-based habits highly effective
- Environmental design can be comprehensive
- Simplified scheduling (no coordination needed)
- Focus on personal lifestyle integration

### Multi-Person Household Maintenance
**Characteristics**: Shared items, conflicting systems, coordination required

**Optimal Approach**:
- Shared habit design (all members participate)
- Shared environmental cues (visible systems for all)
- Coordination meetings (weekly sync on maintenance)
- Individual + shared habit systems (personal + communal)

### Family With Children Maintenance
**Characteristics**: High accumulation, rapid change, limited child participation

**Optimal Approach**:
- Age-appropriate habit systems (simple for younger kids)
- Parent-led maintenance with child participation
- Rotation systems (toys, clothes seasonally)
- Simplified rules for children (one-in-one-out on toys)

### Small Space Maintenance
**Characteristics**: Limited storage, high visual clutter risk, rapid overwhelm

**Optimal Approach**:
- Strict one-in-one-out enforcement
- Vertical storage maximization
- Daily non-negotiable habits (no recovery possible in small space)
- Regular editing (monthly, not quarterly)

### Large Space Maintenance
**Characteristics**: Clutter can hide, multiple rooms, deferred maintenance possible

**Optimal Approach**:
- Room-by-room maintenance rotation
- Preventive maintenance in less-used spaces
- Seasonal deep cleaning integrated with clutter maintenance
- Storage inventory system (prevent "out of sight, out of mind")
