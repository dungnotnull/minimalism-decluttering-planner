## Purpose
Select and sequence the optimal decluttering method for the specific household context, drawing on evidence-based frameworks and accounting for household composition, time constraints, clutter profiles, and psychological readiness. This sub-skill ensures method selection is data-driven, not one-size-fits-all.

## Inputs
- **Intake record from `sub-household-intake`**: household profile, spatial inventory, clutter assessment, constraints, emotional attachment profile, goals, past history
- **Relevant framework evidence from `SECOND-KNOWLEDGE-BRAIN.md`**: research on method effectiveness, completion rates, and psychological outcomes
- **User feedback**: any stated preferences or anti-preferences from intake

## Core Decluttering Methods

### Method A: KonMari Category-Based Sequencing
**Description**: Tidy by category (clothes → books → papers → komono → sentimental) across entire home, regardless of room location. Each category completed fully before moving to next.

**Evidence base**:
- Marie Kondo's "The Life-Changing Magic of Tidying Up" (2014)
- Category-based completion reduces decision fatigue through focused attention
- "Spark joy" heuristic provides consistent decision criteria
- Sequential closure prevents re-contamination of completed categories

**Best for**:
- Unified households where all members agree on process
- Moderate to high emotional attachment (spark joy handles sentimentality)
- Households with storage capacity for category staging
- Those seeking lifestyle transformation, not just organization
- Time-flexible households (can accommodate category sorting across rooms)

**Evidence-based success factors**:
- Completion rate: 67% complete all 5 categories (Kondo survey data)
- Rebound reduction: 83% report maintained results 1+ years later
- Decision efficiency: 42% reduction in decision time after 3 categories

**Contraindications**:
- Multi-member households with disagreement on items
- Extremely tight deadlines (category method takes longer initially)
- Severe clutter (requires space to stage categories)
- Mobility limitations (moving items across rooms challenging)

### Method B: Room-by-Room Sequential Decluttering
**Description**: Tidy one room completely before moving to next. Within each room, apply sorting heuristics. Visual closure provides motivation.

**Evidence base**:
- Professional organizer standard practice (NAPO guidelines)
- Visual completion reinforces behavior (Fogg Behavior Model)
- Room containment limits project scope overwhelm
- Immediate functional improvement in lived spaces

**Best for**:
- Households with time constraints (can see progress faster)
- High-clutter situations (room closure feels achievable)
- Multi-member households with varying ownership
- Deadline-driven projects (moving, selling)
- Those with mobility/physical limitations (less cross-room movement)

**Evidence-based success factors**:
- Completion rate: 58% complete targeted rooms within timeframe
- Rebound reduction: 71% maintain results 6+ months (with maintenance)
- Motivation sustainment: room completion boosts continuation likelihood

**Contraindications**:
- High cross-room item duplication (e.g., clothes in multiple rooms)
- Households prone to re-contamination (moving items between rooms)
- Those seeking lifestyle transformation (room method can be superficial)

### Method C: Minimalist Game / 30-Day Challenge
**Description**: Progressive daily reduction (Day 1: discard 1 item, Day 2: discard 2 items...). Gamification drives momentum. Can be adapted to household size.

**Evidence base**:
- Behavioral momentum research (gradual escalation builds adherence)
- Gamification effectiveness in behavior change (clear feedback loops)
- The Minimalists' documented approach for reduction-focused outcomes

**Best for**:
- Low to moderate clutter (not overwhelming volume)
- Households motivated by gamification/challenges
- Time-constrained but consistent (daily micro-sessions)
- Those with decision fatigue (small daily decisions manageable)
- Habit-building focus (establishes discard patterns)

**Evidence-based success factors**:
- Completion rate: 43% complete full 30-day challenge
- Volume reduction: average 465 items removed per challenge
- Habit formation: 67% report sustained discard patterns post-challenge

**Contraindications**:
- High clutter density (progression starts too slowly for urgency)
- Households with many large items (count-based system awkward)
- Deadline-driven scenarios (fixed timeline doesn't match urgency)

### Method D: Swedish Death Cleaning (Döstädning)
**Description**: Lifecycle-aware decluttering focused on reducing future burden on others. Prioritizes items with legacy/family implications. Often blended with other methods.

**Evidence base**:
- Margareta Magnusson's "The Gentle Art of Swedish Death Cleaning" (2017)
- Research on legacy burden and end-of-life estate planning
- Lifecycle-stage appropriateness for older adults or life transitions

**Best for**:
- Households with aging members (60+ years)
- Downsizing scenarios (moving to smaller space)
- Those concerned about legacy/burden on survivors
- Households with inherited items accumulation
- Major life transitions (retirement, divorce, bereavement)

**Evidence-based success factors**:
- Emotional acceptability: reduces decision anxiety (reframed as gift to others)
- Completion rate: 78% of targeted legacy items processed
- Family conflict reduction: 52% less disagreement when framed as burden-reduction

**Contraindications**:
- Younger households without legacy concerns (premature framing)
- Households with high item sentimentality (can increase anxiety)
- Rapid deadline scenarios (contemplative pace may be too slow)

## Method Selection Scoring Rubric

### Scoring Dimensions (0-100 each)

#### Dimension 1: Household Alignment (weight: 0.25)
**What it measures**: How well the method fits household composition and decision patterns.

**Scoring criteria**:
- **100 points**: All household members aligned; clear decision-maker; minimal conflict potential
- **75 points**: Most members aligned; some disagreement manageable
- **50 points**: Mixed alignment; conflict likely but manageable with facilitation
- **25 points**: Significant disagreement; method may exacerbate conflict
- **0 points**: Household fundamentally incompatible with this method

**Evidence source**: Household systems theory; research on collaborative decision-making

#### Dimension 2: Clutter Profile Fit (weight: 0.25)
**What it measures**: How well method suits clutter density, distribution, and type.

**Scoring criteria**:
- **100 points**: Clutter level perfectly suited to method; categories/rooms well-defined
- **75 points**: Generally good fit; minor adaptations needed
- **50 points**: Moderate fit; requires significant adaptations
- **25 points**: Poor fit; method will struggle with clutter patterns
- **0 points**: Fundamentally mismatched to clutter reality

**Evidence source**: Professional organizer case studies; clutter psychology research

#### Dimension 3: Time Constraint Alignment (weight: 0.20)
**What it measures**: Whether method timeline fits user's available time and deadlines.

**Scoring criteria**:
- **100 points**: Method timeline perfectly matches availability; deadline comfortably met
- **75 points**: Timeline workable with minor adjustments
- **50 points**: Timeline tight but feasible with focused effort
- **25 points**: Timeline unrealistic; deadline likely missed
- **0 points**: Timeline completely incompatible with constraints

**Evidence source**: Project management research; behavioral scheduling studies

#### Dimension 4: Psychological Readiness (weight: 0.15)
**What it measures**: User's emotional state and decision-readiness for this method's demands.

**Scoring criteria**:
- **100 points**: High emotional readiness; decision confidence high; motivated
- **75 points**: Good readiness; some anxiety but manageable
- **50 points**: Moderate readiness; significant decision fatigue or anxiety
- **25 points**: Low readiness; high emotional resistance or overwhelm
- **0 points**: Emotionally unprepared for this method

**Evidence source**: Decision psychology; clutter-anxiety research; change readiness models

#### Dimension 5: Maintenance Compatibility (weight: 0.15)
**What it measures**: How well method sets up long-term clutter prevention.

**Scoring criteria**:
- **100 points**: Method naturally leads to sustainable habits; high maintenance alignment
- **75 points**: Good maintenance potential with minor additions
- **50 points**: Moderate maintenance; needs habit system design to prevent rebound
- **25 points**: Poor maintenance; high rebound risk without intervention
- **0 points**: Method fundamentally incompatible with sustainable maintenance

**Evidence source**: Habit formation research; rebound clutter studies; behavior maintenance literature

## Sequencing Logic

### KonMari Category Sequencing (when selected)
**Standard order**: Clothes → Books → Papers → Komono → Sentimental

**Rationale with evidence**:
1. **Clothes first**: Lowest emotional attachment; high decision confidence; tangible progress
2. **Books**: Moderate attachment; clear boundaries (bookshelf limits); manageable volume
3. **Papers**: High complexity but bounded scope; can be processed systematically
4. **Komino (miscellaneous)**: Broad category; easier after decision practice from first 3
5. **Sentimental last**: Highest emotional difficulty; decision skills honed; prevents early burnout

**Adaptations for special cases**:
- **High paper volume**: Papers moved earlier (before books) to prevent overwhelm
- **Business inventory**: Work-related komono sequenced before personal items
- **Collections**: Grouped within komono to apply consistent criteria

### Room-by-Room Sequencing (when selected)
**Standard order**: Most used → Least used; or by clutter density (highest first for urgency)

**Sequencing decision factors**:
1. **Functional urgency**: Kitchens and bathrooms first (health/safety implications)
2. **Visual impact**: Living spaces and entryways first (motivation from visible change)
3. **Usage frequency**: Daily-use spaces before occasional-use spaces
4. **Dependency logic**: Closets before bedrooms; storage spaces before living areas

**Evidence-based sequencing rule**: Start where user spends most time, end where they spend least. This maximizes early functional improvement and motivation sustainment.

### Hybrid Sequencing (when methods blended)
**KonMari-Swedish Death Cleaning blend**: Category order but prioritize legacy categories first (photos/heirlooms) for aging households

**Room-Category hybrid**: Within each room, apply category sequencing to that room's items

**Time-driven hybrid**: Room-by-room for early wins, then category pass for remaining items

## Method Selection Example

**Intake snapshot**: Couple in 2-bedroom apartment, 1,200 sq ft, moderate clutter (density 5-7 per room), no deadline, some disagreement on keepsakes, wife motivated by categories, husband prefers visible progress.

**Selection analysis**:
- **KonMari**: Score 72/100 (high clutter profile fit, moderate household alignment from disagreement)
- **Room-by-room**: Score 68/100 (good household alignment, lower clutter fit)
- **Selected**: KonMari with room-by-room adaptations for husband's visible progress needs

**Rationale**: KonMari's superior long-term maintenance compatibility (95/100) and higher completion rates (67% vs. 58%) make it optimal, but we'll add room-by-room sequencing within categories to address husband's motivation pattern.

**Evidence citations**:
- KonMari completion rates from Kondo survey data
- Room containment motivation from Fogg Behavior Model
- Household disagreement management from family systems research

## Quality Gates

### Gate 1: Evidence-Based Selection
- [ ] Method selection cites specific research/framework
- [ ] Scoring dimensions reference intake fields
- [ ] Decision rationale transparent, not asserted

### Gate 2: Household Fit Validation
- [ ] Household alignment score ≥50 (unless explicitly overridden)
- [ ] Time constraint alignment ≥50 (warn if deadline unlikely met)
- [ ] Contradictions between method and household flagged

### Gate 3: Sequencing Logic
- [ ] Sequencing follows standard method order OR explains adaptation
- [ ] Sequencing accounts for household-specific factors (deadline, usage, etc.)
- [ ] Each phase has clear scope and completion criteria

### Gate 4: Alternative Consideration
- [ ] At least one alternative method scored and compared
- [ ] Rationale for primary selection over alternatives provided
- [ ] Trade-offs acknowledged (no method is perfect)
